# Technical Analysis Basics

## What technical analysis is
Technical analysis studies price and volume charts to spot patterns and trends, on the
idea that price history can hint at future movement. It differs from fundamental
analysis, which studies the underlying business. Many traders use both together.

## Trends: support and resistance
A trend is the general direction of a price. An uptrend makes higher highs and higher
lows; a downtrend makes lower highs and lower lows. Support is a price level where
buying tends to stop a fall; resistance is a level where selling tends to cap a rise.
When price breaks through support or resistance, traders watch for a new move.

## Moving averages
A moving average smooths price over a period to show the trend. A simple moving average
(SMA) averages closing prices; an exponential moving average (EMA) weights recent
prices more. The 50-day and 200-day averages are widely watched. A "golden cross" (the
50-day crossing above the 200-day) is seen as bullish, and a "death cross" (the 50-day
crossing below) as bearish.

## Relative Strength Index (RSI)
The RSI is a momentum indicator scaled from 0 to 100. A reading above 70 is often
considered overbought (the price may have risen too fast), and below 30 is considered
oversold (it may have fallen too far). Traders use it to gauge whether a move is
stretched.

## Candlestick charts
A candlestick shows the open, high, low, and close for a period. A filled or red candle
means the price closed lower than it opened; a hollow or green candle means it closed
higher. Patterns like a "doji" (open and close nearly equal) can signal indecision.

## Volume
Volume confirms moves: a breakout on high volume is considered more reliable than one on
low volume, because it shows broad participation. Technical analysis is debated and not
foolproof; it describes probabilities, not certainties.
