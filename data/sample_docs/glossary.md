# Investing Glossary

## Ticker symbol
A ticker is the short code that identifies a publicly traded company on an exchange —
for example, AAPL for Apple or MSFT for Microsoft. You use the ticker to look up a
quote or place a trade.

## Bull and bear markets
A bull market is a period of generally rising prices and optimism; a bear market is a
prolonged decline, commonly defined as a drop of 20% or more from recent highs. "Bull"
and "bear" also describe an investor's outlook — bullish means expecting gains, bearish
means expecting declines.

## Stocks, ETFs, and mutual funds
A stock is a share of ownership in a single company. A mutual fund pools money from many
investors to buy a basket of securities, priced once per day. An exchange-traded fund
(ETF) is similar to a mutual fund but trades like a stock throughout the day, and index
ETFs like those tracking the S&P 500 are a popular low-cost way to diversify.

## Index
An index measures the performance of a group of stocks. The S&P 500 tracks 500 large
U.S. companies, the Dow Jones Industrial Average tracks 30 large companies, and the
Nasdaq Composite is heavily weighted toward technology. You cannot buy an index
directly, but you can buy funds that track it.

## Blue-chip stock
A blue-chip is a large, well-established, financially sound company with a long track
record, often paying dividends. These are generally considered lower-risk holdings.

## Liquidity and volatility
Liquidity is how easily an asset can be bought or sold without moving its price;
high-volume stocks are very liquid. Volatility is how much and how quickly a price
moves — higher volatility means bigger swings and more risk.

## Bid, ask, and spread
The bid is the highest price a buyer will pay; the ask is the lowest price a seller will
accept. The difference between them is the spread. Tighter spreads usually mean a more
liquid, actively traded stock.

## IPO
An initial public offering (IPO) is when a private company first sells shares to the
public and begins trading on an exchange.

## Short selling and margin
Short selling is borrowing shares to sell now and buy back later, betting the price will
fall. Margin is borrowing money from your broker to invest; it can amplify gains but
also losses, and is considered high-risk.
