# Investing and Taxes (U.S. basics)

## Capital gains
A capital gain is the profit from selling an investment for more than you paid. You
generally owe tax on a gain only when you sell — unrealized gains on holdings you still
own are not taxed.

## Short-term vs long-term
If you hold an asset for one year or less before selling, the profit is a short-term
capital gain, taxed as ordinary income at your regular income tax rate. If you hold it
for more than one year, it is a long-term capital gain, which is taxed at lower rates.
This is why holding for more than a year can meaningfully reduce the tax you owe.

## The wash sale rule
The wash sale rule prevents claiming a tax loss if you buy the same or a substantially
identical security within 30 days before or after the sale. If you trigger a wash sale,
the disallowed loss is added to the cost basis of the new shares rather than deducted
immediately.

## Dividends
Qualified dividends are taxed at the lower long-term capital gains rates, while
ordinary (non-qualified) dividends are taxed as regular income. Whether a dividend
qualifies depends mainly on how long you held the stock.

## Tax-advantaged accounts
Retirement accounts can reduce or defer taxes. A traditional 401(k) or IRA is funded
with pre-tax money and taxed on withdrawal, while a Roth account is funded with
after-tax money and grows tax-free, with qualified withdrawals not taxed. Contribution
limits are set by the IRS and change each year.
