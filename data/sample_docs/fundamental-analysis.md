# Fundamental Analysis Basics

## What fundamental analysis is
Fundamental analysis estimates a company's true, or intrinsic, value by studying its
business and financial statements, rather than its chart. The goal is to decide whether
a stock is undervalued or overvalued relative to what the company is actually worth.

## The income statement
The income statement shows performance over a period. It starts with revenue (total
sales), subtracts costs and expenses, and ends with net income, also called earnings or
the "bottom line." Growing revenue and net income over time is a healthy sign. Earnings
per share (EPS) comes from this statement.

## The balance sheet
The balance sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns and owes at a point in time. It
lists assets (what the company owns), liabilities (what it owes), and shareholders'
equity (assets minus liabilities). A company with manageable debt relative to its assets
and equity is generally on firmer footing.

## The cash flow statement
The cash flow statement tracks actual cash moving in and out, split into operating,
investing, and financing activities. Free cash flow — the cash left after running and
maintaining the business — is a key measure of financial health, because a company can
report profits yet still run short of cash.

## Key things to look at
Analysts watch revenue growth, profit margins, debt levels, and free cash flow, and
compare valuation metrics like the P/E ratio to competitors. No single figure is enough;
they are read together.

## Where to find the numbers
Public companies must file these statements with regulators. The annual report (10-K)
and quarterly report (10-Q) on SEC EDGAR are the authoritative sources.

## Growth vs value investing
Growth investors seek companies expanding quickly, often accepting high valuations.
Value investors seek solid companies trading below their intrinsic worth. Both rely on
fundamental analysis, but weigh the numbers differently.
