# Notable Large-Cap Stocks (reference examples)

> These are examples of well-known, widely held companies for learning how to research
> stocks. They are NOT recommendations. "Top" companies change constantly — always check
> a financial site for current prices, rankings, and figures before making decisions.

## The mega-cap technology group
A handful of very large technology companies, sometimes called the "Magnificent Seven,"
have made up a large share of the S&P 500's value in the mid-2020s:

- **Apple (AAPL)** — consumer electronics (iPhone, Mac) and services.
- **Microsoft (MSFT)** — software, cloud computing (Azure), and productivity tools.
- **Alphabet (GOOGL)** — Google search, advertising, YouTube, and cloud.
- **Amazon (AMZN)** — e-commerce and Amazon Web Services (AWS) cloud.
- **Nvidia (NVDA)** — graphics and AI accelerator chips.
- **Meta Platforms (META)** — Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp advertising.
- **Tesla (TSLA)** — electric vehicles and energy storage.

## Other widely followed large-caps
- **Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B)** — Warren Buffett's diversified holding company.
- **JPMorgan Chase (JPM)** — the largest U.S. bank by assets.
- **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)** — healthcare and pharmaceuticals, a classic blue-chip.
- **Visa (V)** — global payments network.
- **Exxon Mobil (XOM)** — energy and oil.

## Popular index funds (instant diversification)
Rather than picking single stocks, many investors buy funds that hold the whole market:

- **SPY** and **VOO** — ETFs that track the S&P 500 (500 large U.S. companies).
- **QQQ** — tracks the Nasdaq-100, heavily weighted toward technology.
- **VTI** — tracks essentially the entire U.S. stock market.

## How to evaluate any of these
For any stock, look at its valuation metrics (P/E ratio, market cap, EPS), its dividend
yield if it pays one, its 52-week range, and how it compares to competitors in the same
sector. No single number tells the whole story — use several together, and read the
company's filings.
