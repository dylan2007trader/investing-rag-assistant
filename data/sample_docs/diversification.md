# Diversification and Portfolio Basics

## What diversification is
Diversification means spreading investments across many holdings so that no single one
can sink your portfolio. The idea is captured by the phrase "don't put all your eggs in
one basket." A diversified portfolio aims to reduce risk without necessarily reducing
expected long-term return.

## Correlation
Diversification works best when holdings have low correlation, meaning they do not all
move together. Combining assets that respond differently to the same event smooths out
the overall ride: when one falls, another may hold steady or rise. Owning ten tech
stocks that all move together is far less diversified than owning assets across
different sectors and types.

## Asset classes
Spreading money across asset classes such as stocks, bonds, real estate, and cash
further reduces risk, because these classes often behave differently in the same
economic conditions. Bonds, for example, are typically less volatile than stocks.

## Index funds
Index funds and ETFs give instant diversification by holding hundreds or thousands of
stocks in a single fund that tracks a market index like the S&P 500. They are a common,
low-cost way for beginners to diversify.

## Dollar-cost averaging
Dollar-cost averaging means investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless
of price. It buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when high, and it removes
the temptation to time the market.

## Rebalancing
Over time, winners grow to take up more of your portfolio than intended. Rebalancing
means periodically selling a bit of what has grown and buying what has shrunk to return
to your target mix, which enforces "buy low, sell high" automatically.
