# Financial Research Sites and Tools

## Free quote and news sites
- **Yahoo Finance** — free real-time-ish quotes, charts, news, analyst estimates, and
  company financial statements. A common starting point for everyday research.
- **Google Finance** — simple, clean quotes, charts, and news; good for quick lookups
  and building a basic watchlist.
- **CNBC** and **MarketWatch** — market news, commentary, and coverage of earnings and
  economic events.

## Deeper research and ratings
- **Morningstar** — independent research, star ratings, and analysis, especially strong
  for mutual funds and ETFs.
- **Bloomberg** — professional-grade news and data; the paid Bloomberg Terminal is an
  industry standard, but Bloomberg.com offers free news.
- **Finviz** — a fast, free stock screener and market heatmap for filtering stocks by
  metrics like P/E, market cap, and sector.

## Official filings
- **SEC EDGAR** — the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's official database of
  company filings. This is where you read the primary-source documents: the annual
  report (10-K), quarterly report (10-Q), and material-event report (8-K). It is the
  most authoritative source for a company's financials.

## Charting and education
- **TradingView** — advanced interactive charts and technical-analysis tools, with a
  large free tier.
- **Investopedia** — a widely used reference for definitions and explanations of
  investing terms and concepts.

## Brokerage platforms
Brokerages such as **Fidelity**, **Charles Schwab**, **Vanguard**, and **Robinhood**
provide the account you actually trade through, and most also bundle research, screeners,
analyst reports, and educational content for their customers.

## How to combine them
A typical workflow: find and screen ideas on Finviz or Yahoo Finance, read the deeper
analysis on Morningstar, verify the numbers in the company's own filings on SEC EDGAR,
and place the trade through your brokerage. Cross-checking sources protects you from
relying on any single, possibly biased, opinion.
