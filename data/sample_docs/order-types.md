# Order Types

## Market order
A market order buys or sells a security immediately at the best available current
price. It prioritizes speed of execution over price, so the final price can differ
slightly from the last quoted price, especially for fast-moving or thinly traded
stocks.

## Limit order
A limit order sets the maximum price you are willing to pay when buying, or the
minimum price you are willing to accept when selling. It will only execute at your
limit price or better. This gives you price control but does not guarantee the order
fills if the market never reaches your price.

## Stop order (stop-loss)
A stop order, often called a stop-loss, becomes a market order once the price reaches
a specified "stop" level. Investors use a stop-loss to limit a potential loss: for
example, setting a stop 10% below your purchase price so the position is sold
automatically if it falls that far.

## Stop-limit order
A stop-limit order combines the two: once the stop price is reached, it places a limit
order instead of a market order. This protects you from selling at an unexpectedly low
price, but the order may not fill if the price gaps past your limit.

## Trailing stop
A trailing stop follows the price by a set amount or percentage. If the stock rises,
the stop rises with it; if the stock falls by the trailing amount, the order triggers.
It lets profits run while still capping downside.

## Order duration
A "day" order expires at the end of the trading day if it does not fill. A
"good-till-canceled" (GTC) order stays active until it executes or you cancel it,
often up to 60 or 90 days depending on the broker.
