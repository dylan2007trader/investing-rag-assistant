# Economic Indicators and the Market

## Why the macro picture matters
Individual stocks are affected by the broader economy. A handful of economic indicators
move markets because they shape expectations about growth, inflation, and interest
rates. Investors watch them closely, especially around scheduled release dates.

## Interest rates and the Federal Reserve
The Federal Reserve (the U.S. central bank) sets a key short-term interest rate to
manage the economy. When the Federal Reserve raises rates, borrowing gets more expensive,
which tends to slow the economy and can pressure stock prices; when it lowers rates,
borrowing is cheaper and stocks often rise. Rate decisions are among the most
market-moving events.

## Inflation (CPI)
Inflation is the rate at which prices rise over time, most commonly tracked by the
Consumer Price Index (CPI). High inflation erodes purchasing power and often pushes the
Federal Reserve to raise rates, which markets usually dislike. Low, stable inflation is
generally seen as healthy.

## GDP
Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measures the total value of goods and services a country
produces, and is the broadest gauge of economic growth. Rising GDP signals expansion; two
consecutive quarters of shrinking GDP is a common informal definition of a recession.

## Jobs and unemployment
The monthly jobs report and the unemployment rate show the health of the labor market.
Strong hiring signals a growing economy but can also stoke inflation fears; rising
unemployment signals weakness. Markets often react sharply to surprises in this data.

## Consumer confidence and earnings season
Consumer confidence surveys gauge how optimistic people feel about spending. During
"earnings season," when public companies report quarterly results, individual stocks can
move sharply on how results compare to expectations. Macro data sets the backdrop;
earnings drive individual names.
