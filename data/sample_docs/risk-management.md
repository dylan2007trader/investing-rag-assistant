# Risk Management

## Position sizing
Position sizing is deciding how much money to put into a single trade or holding. A
common guideline is to risk only a small percentage of your total portfolio on any one
position, so that a single loss cannot do serious damage. Many investors keep any one
position well under 5 percent of the portfolio.

## The 1 percent rule
A widely used risk rule is to risk no more than 1 percent of your account on a single
trade. If your account is $10,000, that means structuring the trade so a stop-loss
would cap the loss at about $100. This keeps any one bad trade small relative to the
whole account.

## Risk/reward ratio
The risk/reward ratio compares how much you stand to lose to how much you stand to
gain. A 1:3 risk/reward means risking $1 to potentially make $3. Favoring trades with
a higher reward than risk means you can be right less than half the time and still come
out ahead over many trades.

## Stop-losses
A stop-loss automatically exits a position at a preset price to cap a loss. Deciding
your exit before you enter removes emotion from the decision and enforces discipline.

## Only risk what you can afford to lose
A core principle is never investing money you may need in the short term or cannot
afford to lose. Money for rent, bills, or an emergency fund should not be exposed to
market risk.
