# Valuation Metrics and Ratios

## Market capitalization
Market cap is the total value of a company's shares: share price multiplied by the
number of shares outstanding. Companies are grouped as large-cap (roughly $10 billion
or more), mid-cap (about $2–10 billion), and small-cap (under about $2 billion).
Large-caps are generally more stable; small-caps are more volatile but can grow faster.

## Earnings per share (EPS)
EPS is a company's net profit divided by its number of shares outstanding. It shows how
much profit is attributed to each share. Rising EPS over time is a sign of improving
profitability.

## Price-to-earnings ratio (P/E)
The P/E ratio is the share price divided by earnings per share. It tells you how much
investors are paying for each dollar of the company's earnings. A high P/E can mean the
market expects strong future growth, or that the stock is expensive; a low P/E can
signal a bargain or a struggling company. P/E is most useful when comparing companies
in the same industry. "Trailing" P/E uses the past 12 months of earnings, while
"forward" P/E uses estimated future earnings.

## PEG ratio
The PEG ratio divides the P/E ratio by the company's expected earnings growth rate. It
adjusts the P/E for growth, so a fast-growing company with a high P/E may still look
reasonably priced. A PEG around 1 is often considered fair value.

## Price-to-book ratio (P/B)
The P/B ratio compares the share price to the company's book value (assets minus
liabilities) per share. A P/B under 1 means the stock trades for less than its
accounting net worth, which value investors sometimes look for.

## Dividend yield
Dividend yield is the annual dividend per share divided by the share price, shown as a
percentage. A stock paying $2 a year at a $100 price has a 2% yield. Higher yields
provide more income but can sometimes signal a struggling company.

## Beta
Beta measures how much a stock moves relative to the overall market. A beta of 1 moves
with the market; above 1 is more volatile than the market; below 1 is less volatile. A
beta of 1.5 tends to move 50% more than the market in either direction.

## 52-week range and volume
The 52-week high and low show the highest and lowest prices over the past year, giving
context for the current price. Volume is the number of shares traded in a period; high
volume means more activity and usually easier buying and selling (liquidity).
